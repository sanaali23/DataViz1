Resettlement Deeply

Michigan: The Promised Land for Iraqi Refugees

Why would Iraqi refugees continue to move to Michigan when resettlement efforts are slowing and jobs are getting increasingly hard to find?

Written byDanish Mehboob Published on Apr. 21, 2017 Read time Approx. 3 minutes

Framing the issue: why would Iraqis or anyone go to Michigan?

Despite Michigan offering limited job opportunities and restricting resettlement to incoming refugees, Iraqi refugees still flock to the state in hope of finding community and culture. According to Ann Corcoran, editor of Refugee Resettlement Watch, 29141 refugees were resettled in Michigan in the last ten years, and 18505 of them were Iraqis. Mohamad Soleiman, an Iraqi refugee living in upstate New York, says that he has wanted to move to Michigan for a long time too, but it's risky. His job prospects are uncertain, especially with the state's economic decline.

Iraqis are still arriving despite the difficult conditions to resettle in Michigan

However, when Soleiman visited Dearborn, Michigan he says it felt like home. A feeling that he is still trying to find in Albany, New York. Joseph Kassab, a member of the Iraqi Christian Advocacy and Empowerment Council, says that Iraqis will always prefer to live together and that the refugees are doing well because of the community around them.

The state of the economy in Michigan

According to the Department of Labor Statistics, employment took a steep dive around 2007 till 2011. From a little over 800000, employment fell to about 720000. This was around the same time Iraqi resettlement started slowing in Michigan, and had its steepest drop in 2007. According to Madiha Tariq, Deputy Director at Arab Community Center for Economic and Social Services, it's still difficult for newly arriving refugees to find employment, but prospects are improving. In 2013, the Community Center connected over 2000 people with jobs throughout metro Detroit. And the numbers have kept steady, according to Tariq.

Conditions leading to slowed resettlement of Iraqis

The State Department limited Michigan's resettlement efforts in June of 2008 by only allowing refugees who have immediate family in the area to resettle. The new policy came after resettlement agents were finding it increasingly difficult to place newly arriving refugees in jobs and housing within the first three months of resettlement. This policy change was an effort to stem the influx of Iraqis to Detroit, especially areas that already have significant Iraqi populations like Dearborn and Sterling Heights.

The newly arriving Iraqi refugees, since the mid-2000s who first settled in other states became domestic secondary migrants after moving to Michigan within the first couple months of being in the U.S. This has become a trend, according to Ahmad Jaber, Director of the Arab American Association of New York, which caters to the second-largest Arab population in the U.S. Jaber keeps ties with the other large Arab associations in the U.S. too. And metro Detroit has the largest Arab population in the U.S. according to the Census Bureau. Most Iraqis live in metro Detroit, where the Muslim community is approximately 12,000 and there are about 100,000 Chaldeans, Christian Catholics from Iraq, according to Kassab from the Iraqi Christian Council. The Institute is a non-profit serving Michigan's Iraqis, the largest population of Chaldeans outside of Iraq.

Hardships

Those domestic secondary migrants relinquish any financial assistance from the State Department after moving to Michigan. Mostly because the governmental supports do not follow them as they are specifically distributed to the resettlement agency of the refugee's original resettlement destination. And stipends are not the only form of assistance left behind, but the refugees moving to Michigan give up on health care, food stamps, and educational opportunities too.

How are the secondary migrants surviving?

However, resettlement conditions are starting to improve, including employment opportunities. There are various non-profit organizations trying to help the already large and growing Iraqi population like the Arab Community Center and the Chaldean Federation. These non-profits reach out to community members in the area to set up job fairs, donation drives, and even provide transport, which tends to be an issue in metro Detroit. [TK.
